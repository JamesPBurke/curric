Student Unit Overview

Overview of the Micro:bit hardware unit, from switches to foam cars.

Course: Engineering Design

Teacher: Mr. Burke

Unit Length: Approximately 8 weeks

Starting Point: You already know the very basics of Micro:bit programming. Now we use that knowledge with real hardware.

Unit Overview

In this unit, you will move from simple physical computing to a full engineering build. First, you will design and build a working switch that connects to a Micro:bit. Then, you will use what you learned about wiring, inputs, outputs, and troubleshooting to help build a foam-bodied car controlled by a Micro:bit.

This is not just a coding unit. It is also a design, fabrication, and problem-solving unit. You will write code, test circuits, carve foam, mount parts, troubleshoot movement, and improve your work over time.

Essential Question

How can we use code, circuits, and physical design together to build working systems?

Major Projects

Project 1: Switch + Signal Mini-Project

You will build a physical switch out of simple materials such as cardboard and aluminum foil. The switch will connect to the Micro:bit and trigger an output.

Possible outputs include an icon or animation on the Micro:bit display, an external LED turning on, or a blinking signal pattern.

This project will help you learn GPIO pins, open and closed circuits, how to connect physical input to programmed output, and how to make a prototype more reliable.

Project 2: Foam Car Final

You will design and build a foam-bodied car with a Micro:bit as the control system. The car will need to be physically built, wired, and programmed.

The first version of the car project is focused on making a working vehicle that can move intentionally under Micro:bit control. Some groups may also experiment with more advanced features if time and materials allow.

This project will help you learn safe wiring and testing, servo control, physical design and fabrication, mounting and organizing components, troubleshooting code, power, and mechanical systems.

What You Will Practice

  • identify important Micro:bit pins and explain what they do
  • build a basic hardware circuit using inputs and outputs
  • write MakeCode programs that respond to external hardware
  • explain the difference between signal wiring and power wiring
  • test and troubleshoot a servo-controlled mechanism
  • design and improve a physical prototype
  • explain engineering decisions using evidence from testing

What Success Looks Like

A successful student in this unit tests frequently instead of guessing, makes one change at a time when troubleshooting, documents failures and improvements, builds carefully and safely, and explains how the code, wiring, and physical design work together.

How You Will Be Assessed

You will be assessed on functionality, engineering process and revision, code quality, build quality and craftsmanship, and explanation and reflection.

Unit Timeline

  • Weeks 1–3: Micro:bit pins, circuits, switch project, and reliability improvements
  • Weeks 4–5: Car planning, servo testing, safe wiring, and early fabrication
  • Weeks 6–7: Car assembly, coding, and testing
  • Week 8: Showcase, reflection, and revision

Materials You May Use

  • Micro:bit boards
  • battery packs
  • wires or alligator clips
  • LEDs and resistors
  • cardboard and aluminum foil
  • pink foam insulation
  • glue and tape
  • wheels, axles, and servo motors

Class Expectations

  • Build safely.
  • Test often.
  • Take care of shared materials.
  • Do not force parts or wiring.
  • Record what changed when something stops working.
  • Ask better questions than “it doesn’t work.”

Useful questions include: Is this a code problem, a wiring problem, a power problem, or a build problem? What worked before? What changed? What is one thing we can test next?

Engineering is not about getting everything right immediately. It is about building, testing, learning, and improving. This unit is designed to make that process visible.
