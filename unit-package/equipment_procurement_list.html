Equipment & Procurement List

Teacher-facing planning and purchasing guide for the Micro:bit unit.

Purpose: Teacher-facing planning document for the first iteration of the Micro:bit hardware unit.

Planning Assumptions

  • students already know basic Micro:bit programming
  • the unit includes a switch mini-project and a foam car final
  • the first iteration prioritizes a working drivetrain over advanced steering
  • yellow continuous-rotation servos are the most important motor purchase
  • blue positional servos are optional extension hardware

Recommended Core Setup Per Team

Electronics

  • 1 Micro:bit
  • 1 USB cable for programming
  • 1 battery pack for the Micro:bit
  • 4–8 alligator clip leads or jumper leads
  • 1 breadboard (recommended)
  • 1 breakout board / edge connector adapter if available
  • 2–4 LEDs
  • resistor assortment

Switch Project Consumables

  • cardboard scraps
  • aluminum foil
  • masking tape and/or electrical tape
  • glue sticks or school-safe adhesive
  • scissors
  • optional: brads, binder clips, clothespins

Car Project Materials

  • pink foam insulation board or blocks
  • foam-safe glue or hot glue system
  • rulers / measuring tools
  • axle material (dowels, skewers, rods, etc.)
  • wheels
  • zip ties, rubber bands, tape, or mounting hardware

Motion and Power

  • 1–2 yellow continuous-rotation servos per team
  • optional: 1 blue positional servo per advanced team or demo station
  • separate battery pack for the servo/motor system
  • spare batteries

Strongly Recommended Teacher Reserve

Reserve electronics

  • 2–4 extra Micro:bits
  • extra USB cables
  • extra battery packs
  • extra alligator clips and jumper wires
  • extra breadboards
  • extra LEDs and resistors

Reserve build materials

  • extra foam blocks
  • backup wheel sets
  • backup axle material
  • replacement servos
  • tape, glue, and fastening materials

Procurement Priorities

Tier 1: Absolutely necessary

  • Micro:bits
  • USB cables
  • battery packs
  • clip leads / jumper wires
  • cardboard, foil, tape
  • pink foam insulation
  • yellow continuous-rotation servos
  • separate motor battery solution
  • wheels and axle materials

Tier 2: Highly recommended

  • breadboards
  • edge-connector adapters / breakout boards
  • extra LEDs and resistors
  • labeled storage bins
  • spare replacement hardware

Tier 3: Nice to have

  • blue positional servos
  • better mounting brackets
  • improved wheels / chassis hardware
  • teacher demo kits
  • printed reference cards and laminated wiring sheets

Recommended Buying Strategy

For a first run of the unit, do not over-purchase exotic hardware. Buy enough for one complete setup per student team, a few replacement parts, and one or two teacher demo rigs.

The safest first-iteration plan is to standardize the servo type where possible, standardize the wheel solution where possible, and standardize the battery solution where possible. Too many hardware variations create instructional chaos.

Suggested Organization Plan

Create labeled bins or bags for:

  • Team electronics kits
  • Switch project consumables
  • Car hardware kits
  • Shared cutting/fabrication tools
  • Teacher-only reserve parts

This will save time every day and reduce the ritual of students claiming that a missing wire is a profound injustice.

Questions to Answer Before Ordering

  1. How many students or teams will build cars?
  2. Will every team get its own car, or will some teams share?
  3. What wheel solution is available or easiest to source?
  4. Will you use breadboards, breakout boards, or direct clip connections?
  5. What battery solution is safest and easiest to manage in your room?
  6. Which fabrication tools are allowed in your setting?

Recommendation

For Iteration 1, optimize for reliability, replaceability, simple wiring, simple mounting, and low drama. Elegant hardware is delightful. Consistent hardware is teachable.
